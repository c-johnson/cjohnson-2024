When I visited Mali in 2008, the experience profoundly affected the trajectory of my life. I initially wanted to use my love of math and science to help people through medicine, but during my internship at the Women and Children’s Hospital in Koutiala I decided that my contribution to Africa would be through energy engineering.

One of the first things I noticed when I arrived in Mali was that everyone had a cell phone and I wondered how this happened in a developing country. I later learned that pay-as-you go cell phones work well in Africa because cell phone technology doesn’t require a massive electrical grid, no landlines, no complicated contracts, and no monthly commitments.

I realized that if Africa could leapfrog landline infrastructure to localized cell phone towers, they could similarly leapfrog fossil fuel power plants to localized renewable energy systems. Renewable energy technologies have an enormous potential to provide energy to thousands of people who would otherwise have no access to electricity.

There are some astounding statistics regarding energy and electricity in the developing world. For example, only 2% of rural Africans have access to electricity. This has a profound impact on their lives and opportunities. Women have to cook everything over open fires and daily walk for an hour or more to obtain drinking water that is often contaminated. Farmers must do everything by hand and doctors have limited options for diagnostic tools and treatments available with no access to electricity.

Renewable energy systems, in addition to being non-polluting, have an advantage over fossil fuels because they can be installed locally, one hospital or school at a time, without needing to be integrated with a large grid. Also, water, wind and sunlight don’t require railroads, highways, and pipelines for fuel transportation. The relatively low cost of investment allows for a rapid expansion of access to electricity.

The pilot project of the World Energy Project is the installation of photovoltaic systems for medical facilities in Mali, West Africa. Many medical facilities lack access to power, and it is very expensive for those lucky enough to have access. For example, the Women and Children’s Hospital in Koutiala currently gets electricity from the grid and spends 22% of their budget on energy costs. The grid is unreliable and expensive, so the World Energy Project is working with Anco Vanbergeijk, a Dutch engineer, and Daniel Thera, a Malian lawyer and philanthropist, to alleviate the burden of energy imposed on Malian medical facilities. We have formed a team of students and professors from the University of Nebraska - Lincoln (UNL) and Creighton University. The Mali Team is composed of six students, including myself, that are traveling to Mali in June 2011. We are all very passionate about applying our engineering education on this installation trip and learning more about what we can do to further help developing areas of the world.

In addition to the our project in Mali, we are partnering with a variety of other nonprofits and nongovernmental organizations to accomplish similar energy projects in other areas of the world. I am excited to use my previous experience in Mali and my engineering education to do something about the limited access to electricity.
